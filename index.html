Our Solution

Clinical Digital Twin is an end-to-end pipeline that unifies MIMIC-IV hospital data, emergency department records, and ECG measurements into a single patient representation for cardiovascular risk prediction. By fusing ECG waveforms, vital signs, and structured clinical features, we enable multi-label diagnosis prediction across 17 cardiovascular ICD-10 categories — establishing a foundation for AI that can support earlier detection and personalized care.

Our model integrates multiple clinical data streams into a unified patient representation to capture a comprehensive view of cardiovascular health. We combine baseline demographic and admission-level features such as age, ethnicity, gender, and race with structured clinical measurements including heart rate, blood pressure, oxygen saturation, and temperature. In addition, we incorporate electrocardiogram data by leveraging both machine-generated measurements such as PR interval, QRS duration, and QTc, along with raw waveform signals to provide high-resolution insight into cardiac electrophysiology. These heterogeneous data sources are standardized through temporal alignment, normalization, missing value handling, and feature encoding to ensure consistency across modalities. Static features are encoded once per admission, while time-series signals are aggregated or modeled using sequential architectures to capture dynamic physiological trends. The result is a multimodal patient-level representation that reflects both baseline risk and real-time cardiac state, enabling robust cardiovascular risk modeling and improved predictive performance.

GroupSplit: Preventing Data Leakage

MIMIC-IV patients often have multiple ED encounters. A naive random split lets the same patient appear in both training and test sets, inflating performance by leaking patient-specific patterns.

GroupSplit solves this by splitting at the subject_id level — all encounters from one patient stay in the same partition. The 70/10/20 split is stratified on the most common diagnosis label to preserve class balance, ensuring evaluation reflects generalization to entirely unseen patients.

75,538 ED encounters → Group by subject_id → Stratified patient-level split → 70% Train / 10% Val / 20% Test

Model Architecture

CardioTwin is a gated multimodal fusion architecture that integrates three clinical data streams — ECG waveforms, vital signs, and structured EHR features — into a unified 128-dimensional patient representation for multi-label cardiovascular diagnosis prediction. Each modality is encoded independently, then combined through a learned gating mechanism that dynamically weights each modality's contribution on a per-patient basis.

ECG Encoder
ECG-FM foundation model
1,536-dim → MLP → 128-dim

Raw 12-lead waveforms are split into two 5-second segments and encoded by ECG-FM, a self-supervised transformer pretrained on millions of ECGs. The two 768-dim segment embeddings are concatenated to form a 1,536-dim representation, then compressed to 128 dimensions via a two-layer MLP with BatchNorm, GELU, and dropout.

Vital Sign Encoder
Summary Stats MLP + LSTM
30 stats + 12 timesteps → 128-dim

Two parallel branches process vitals. An MLP encodes 30 summary statistics (mean, std, min, max across 6 vitals plus 7 nearest-to-ECG features) to 128 dimensions. An LSTM with 64 hidden units processes the raw vital sign time series (up to 12 timesteps) and projects its final hidden state to 128 dimensions. The two outputs are concatenated and fused through a linear layer.

EHR Encoder
Structured clinical features
~47 features → MLP → 128-dim

Demographics (age, gender, race), ECG interval measurements (PR, QRS, QTc), and encounter metadata are encoded by a two-layer MLP to 128 dimensions. Feature dimensionality is determined lazily at runtime to accommodate different input configurations.

Gated Multimodal Fusion

The three 128-dim modality encodings are concatenated into a 384-dim vector and passed through a two-layer gate network that produces three softmax-normalized scalar weights — one per modality. The fused state is the weighted sum of modality encodings, then compressed through a fusion MLP to a 64-dim patient state representation. A no-gate ablation using uniform mean pooling confirmed that the learned gating provides meaningful patient-specific weighting.

3 × 128-dim encodings → 384-dim concat → Gate network → α₁, α₂, α₃ → Weighted sum → Fusion MLP → 64-dim patient state
Classification Head

A linear layer maps the 64-dim fused representation to 17 output logits. Sigmoid activation converts each logit to an independent probability for multi-label diagnosis prediction. The model is trained with BCEWithLogitsLoss using per-label positive class weights (nneg/npos) to address severe class imbalance across rare cardiovascular conditions.

How to Interpret These Results
What ROC-AUC means here

A macro ROC-AUC of 0.907 means the model ranks a true positive case above a true negative case ~91% of the time, averaged across all 17 labels. Values above 0.85 are considered strong for multi-label clinical classification. Per-label AUC matters more than the macro average for individual conditions.

Why F1 is low despite high AUC

Macro F1 of 0.497 reflects severe label imbalance — rare conditions like Pulmonary Embolism or Cardiomyopathy have very few positive cases, driving down precision and recall even when AUC is high. F1 near 0.5 on a 17-label imbalanced task is expected and not a signal of model failure.

What's signal vs. noise

AUC differences >0.01 between models are meaningful given the test set size (24,782 encounters). Differences <0.005 should be treated as noise. Per-label comparisons on rare conditions (<200 positive cases) are less reliable and should be interpreted with caution.

What these results don't show

These metrics are computed on retrospective ED data from a single institution (BIDMC). They do not reflect prospective clinical performance, and the model has not been validated on external patient populations. AUC measures ranking ability, not calibration — predicted probabilities should not be treated as direct risk estimates without further calibration.

Limitations & Failure Modes

We believe honest evaluation requires acknowledging where the model falls short, not just where it succeeds. Below are the most significant limitations of the current system.

Single-Institution Data

All data originates from Beth Israel Deaconess Medical Center (BIDMC) via MIMIC-IV. The model has not been tested on external patient populations. Demographic, equipment, and workflow differences at other institutions may significantly degrade performance.

Rare Condition Underperformance

Conditions with fewer than ~200 positive cases in the training set — such as Pulmonary Embolism, Cardiomyopathy, and some arrhythmia subtypes — have substantially lower per-label AUC and F1. Class weighting and SMOTE partially mitigate this, but these labels remain unreliable for clinical use.

Missing Modality Handling

Not every ED encounter has an associated ECG recording. When ECG data is absent, the ECG encoder outputs a zero-padded embedding. The model was not explicitly trained on missing modality patterns, and performance on ECG-absent encounters may be lower than reported metrics suggest.

ICD-10 Label Noise

Diagnosis codes are assigned at discharge and reflect billing and administrative practice as much as clinical ground truth. Under-coding and over-coding are common, particularly for secondary diagnoses. The model learns from these imperfect labels, which introduces noise into both training and evaluation.

No Prospective Validation

All results are retrospective. The model was trained and evaluated on historical encounter data, not deployed in a live clinical environment. Prospective performance — where patient outcomes are unknown at inference time — may differ, particularly for patients presenting with atypical symptom profiles.

Uncalibrated Probabilities

The sigmoid outputs represent relative risk ranking, not calibrated clinical probabilities. A predicted score of 0.7 does not mean 70% likelihood of that diagnosis. Without explicit probability calibration (e.g., Platt scaling or isotonic regression), outputs should not be presented to clinicians as direct probability estimates.
